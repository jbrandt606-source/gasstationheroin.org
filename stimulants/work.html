High-stim pre-workout is not just caffeine — it is a cocktail of unregulated amphetamine-like stimulants.

Products with names like “Jack3d,” “Crack,” “Condemned,” “Hellboy,” “Addall,” and “Lipodrene Extreme” promise intense energy, focus, and endurance. But the ingredients list often includes DMAA (1,3-dimethylamylamine), DMHA (2-aminoisoheptane), yohimbine, synephrine, and other stimulants that have been banned by the FDA in dietary supplements — yet they still appear in gas stations and online.

These products are marketed to young athletes, bodybuilders, and fitness enthusiasts. A single scoop can contain as much stimulant as multiple cups of coffee plus amphetamine-like compounds. The result: heart attacks, strokes, seizures, and even death — especially in teenagers and young adults.

High-stim pre-workout, explained in plain English

No chemistry degree needed. These are industrial-strength stimulants sold as workout supplements — and they have sent young people to emergency rooms and morgues.

What are DMAA and DMHA?

DMAA (1,3-dimethylamylamine) and DMHA (2-aminoisoheptane) are synthetic stimulants that act like amphetamines. They were originally developed as nasal decongestants, not as workout aids. DMAA was banned by the FDA in 2013 after being linked to numerous deaths, heart attacks, and hemorrhagic strokes — but it still appears in gas station and online pre-workout products under names like “geranium extract” or “methylhexanamine.”

DMHA is a chemical cousin of DMAA and is similarly dangerous. The FDA has issued warning letters about DMHA, but enforcement is slow, and products remain on shelves.

Why should parents be concerned?

Because high-stim pre-workout products are sold in gas stations, vape shops, and supplement stores — often to teenagers who walk in without ID. A 16-year-old athlete can buy a tub of “Crack” or “Jack3d” and take a full scoop without understanding the risks.

  • Heart attacks in teens: There are documented cases of young, healthy people suffering cardiac arrest after taking DMAA-containing pre-workout.
  • Strokes: DMAA is known to cause hemorrhagic (bleeding) strokes by spiking blood pressure.
  • Seizures: High doses of stimulants can trigger seizures, even in people with no history.
  • Mixing with other drugs: Teens often combine pre-workout with energy drinks, caffeine pills, or even ADHD medications — a recipe for stimulant overdose.

Where are these sold?

Gas stations, vape shops, smoke shops, supplement stores (GNC, Vitamin Shoppe), online retailers (Amazon, eBay, independent sites), and even some grocery stores. Brands like “Jack3d,” “Crack” (Dark Labs), “Condemned,” “Hellboy” (Insane Labz), “Addall,” and “Lipodrene Extreme” are easy to find.

What is “Crack” pre-workout?

“Crack” by Dark Labs is an infamous high-stim pre-workout that contains DMAA and other banned stimulants. The name alone is a red flag — it normalizes drug culture and appeals to thrill-seekers. The product has been linked to hospitalizations and has received FDA warning letters, but it is still available in some gas stations and online.

Parents should be alarmed by any product named after an illegal drug, sold as a “dietary supplement.”

What is “Addall”?

“Addall” is a product that mimics the name of the prescription ADHD medication Adderall. It often contains DMHA, yohimbine, and high-dose caffeine — sold in gas stations as a “dietary supplement.” The branding is intentionally deceptive, suggesting it will provide the same focus and energy as prescription amphetamines.

Taking “Addall” could cause severe anxiety, heart palpitations, and dangerous interactions with real ADHD medications.

What are the signs of stimulant overdose?

Symptoms include racing heartbeat, chest pain, shortness of breath, severe headache, confusion, agitation, hallucinations, seizures, and loss of consciousness. If someone has taken high-stim pre-workout and shows these signs, call 911 immediately.

Many young athletes take more than the recommended dose because they want stronger effects. This dramatically increases the risk of overdose.

Are these products legal?

The FDA has banned DMAA as a dietary ingredient, but some manufacturers still sell it by labeling it as something else (“geranium extract” or “methylhexanamine”). DMHA has received FDA warning letters but is not explicitly banned in all cases. Enforcement is inconsistent, so dangerous products remain on shelves.

The legal gray area means parents cannot assume that a product on a gas station shelf has been tested or approved for safety.

What about “Lipodrene Extreme” and weight loss stimulants?

“Lipodrene Extreme” and similar “extreme weight loss” products are often high-stim pre-workout formulas repackaged. They contain yohimbine, synephrine, caffeine, and sometimes DMAA or DMHA. The marketing claims “extreme weight loss” and “energy aid” — but the cardiovascular risks are significant.

Teens and young adults looking to lose weight quickly are especially vulnerable to these dangerous products.

How can parents spot these products?

Look for tubs or bottles with aggressive, extreme branding — words like “crack,” “condemned,” “hellboy,” “jack3d,” “extreme,” “ultra concentrated,” “high stim.” Ingredients to watch for: DMAA, DMHA, 1,3-dimethylamylamine, 2-aminoisoheptane, geranium extract, yohimbine, synephrine, and undisclosed “proprietary stimulant blends.”

If you see these in a gas station or your teen’s room, treat it as a potential health emergency.

What to look for in your neighborhood

Red flags on shelves

  • Tubs or bottles labeled “high stim,” “extreme,” “ultra concentrated,” “pre-workout”
  • Names like “Crack,” “Jack3d,” “Condemned,” “Hellboy,” “Addall,” “Lipodrene Extreme”
  • Ingredients: DMAA, DMHA, 1,3-dimethylamylamine, 2-aminoisoheptane, geranium extract, yohimbine, synephrine
  • “Proprietary stimulant blend” — hiding the actual ingredients and amounts
  • Warnings that say “use only as directed” or “not for use by people with medical conditions” — a sign of danger
  • Sold next to gas station candy, energy shots, and vapes

What communities can do

  • Ask gas station owners: “Do you know that DMAA is banned by the FDA and can cause heart attacks in young people?”
  • Warn schools, athletic coaches, parents, and youth sports organizations about high-stim pre-workout dangers.
  • Report products containing DMAA or DMHA to the FDA MedWatch and the FTC.
  • Take photos of products on shelves — documentation helps regulators and law enforcement.
  • Contact state legislators and health departments to ban gas station sales of high-stim pre-workout.
