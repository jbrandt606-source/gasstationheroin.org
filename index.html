Vape shops & gas stations are selling dangerously addictive compounds with zero safety warnings for families.

GasStationHeroin.org documents real products found on shelves in 2025: tianeptine (“gas station heroin”), high-concentration kratom shots, delta-8 gummies, unlicensed vapes, synthetic stimulants, and more. Parents, educators, and community members — this is what your children see every day.

🛒 On shelves right now

Actual product categories found in smoke shops, vape stores, and gas stations across the US. Used without childproofing, warning labels, or age verification.

Tianeptine

⚠️ EXTREME RISK • “GAS STATION HEROIN”

Marketed as “energy” or “mood supplement” but acts like an opioid. Highly addictive, sold in capsules, tablets, and powders next to cash registers.

FDA warns of seizures & addiction

Kratom Extracts

⚠️ Potent shots & powders

Liquid shots, 50x extracts, and gummies with 20+ mg mitragynine. Often labeled “herbal supplement” with bright colors mimicking energy drinks.

Available at 7-Eleven, Shell, independent vape shops

Delta-8 THC Gummies

⚠️ Unregulated psychoactive

Sour worms, peach rings, packaged like kid-friendly candy. No age verification, high potency, linked to poison control calls in teens.

Appealing packaging + zero childproofing

Disposable Vapes

⚠️ 5000+ puffs, synthetic nicotine

Flavors like cotton candy, blue razz, clear devices designed for minors. Illegal high-nicotine content and heavy metals.

Sold alongside soda & chips — normalizing addiction

Synthetic Cannabinoids

⚠️ “Spice” / “K2” rebranded

Herbal blends and liquids with no ingredient oversight. Causes psychosis, seizures. Hidden behind ‘not for human consumption’ stickers.

Often near actual CBD products to confuse parents

Phenibut & "Nootropic" shots

⚠️ Addiction risk unknown

Sold as focus or sleep shots. Phenibut acts on GABA receptors, leads to severe withdrawal. Displayed next to energy shots.

No warning about dependence or mixing with alcohol

🧠 Signs your teen may encounter these products

Recognize early exposure. Unlabeled vapes, unusual powders, or sudden behavioral changes can indicate gas station/vape shop products.

Hidden in plain sight

Kratom capsules in “herbal” bottles, delta-8 edibles disguised as candy, and vapes that look like USB drives. Check backpacks, rooms, and receipts from local minimarts.

Unexplained mood swings

Sedation, euphoria, or irritability — tianeptine and kratom can cause opioid-like effects. Watch for tiny powder residue, strange empty capsules, or sweet-smelling vape clouds.

Where to spot them

Gas stations near schools, independent tobacco shops, vape lounges, CBD stores, and even corner delis. Walk inside and notice products behind the counter or in open shelving.

Community check-up

Organize a store walk with local parents. Share findings in neighborhood groups. The first step is visibility — demand warning signs or product removal.

⚡ Take action — protect your family & neighborhood

Community awareness changes local retail. Here’s how you can drive change today.

1. Inspect local shops

Take photos of products like tianeptine, kratom extracts, or unlabeled vapes. Submit anonymous reports to local health departments.

2. Demand warning labels

Contact state representatives and city councils. Many parents don’t know these products are unregulated — advocate for placement restrictions.

3. Start the conversation

Talk to your kids about “gas station heroin” and kratom shots. Use real names, show them what packaging looks like — awareness saves lives.
