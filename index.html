Kratom • Parent Guide

“Gas Station Heroin” is Kratom — and its 7‑OH metabolite drives the risk.

Kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) is sold as a “natural” product in gas stations and vape shops. Its alkaloids — mitragynine and the potent metabolite 7‑hydroxymitragynine (7‑OH) — act at opioid receptors, creating dependence, withdrawal, and documented harms. This page curates MAHA’s kratom evidence.

What is Kratom?

Kratom is a leaf from Southeast Asia, sold in capsules, powders, and shots. It’s marketed as a supplement but can behave like an opioid at common doses. Retail products lack dosing standards and often skip age checks.

  • Common names: “K‑shots,” “Maeng Da,” “Mitra.”
  • Where found: vape shops, gas stations, online.
  • Forms: capsules, extracts, flavored drinks.

What is 7‑Hydroxymitragynine (7‑OH)?

7‑OH is a potent active related to kratom that binds mu‑opioid receptors. Some products even advertise “7‑OH” content — a red flag for stronger opioid‑like effects, tolerance, and withdrawal.

Key idea: Shelf‑legal ≠ FDA‑approved. Potency can vary widely and labels can omit or misstate alkaloid levels.

Packaging & Sales Tactics

Bright packaging and candy flavors target impulse buys. Some labels say “not for human consumption” to dodge oversight while being sold for ingestion. Look for terms like “extract,” “enhanced,” or “7‑OH.”

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 7‑OH listed on labels?

Some products reference 7‑OH directly; others only list “extract” or “enhanced.” Absence on the label does not mean absence in the product.

Can kratom be addictive?

Yes. Reports describe opioid‑like tolerance and withdrawal. Severity varies by dose, duration, and product potency.

Is it FDA‑approved?

No. Kratom and 7‑OH are not FDA‑approved for any medical use and are not authorized as dietary ingredients.
