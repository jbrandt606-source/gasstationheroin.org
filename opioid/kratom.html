Kratom: a hidden opioid crisis inside gas stations and vape shops.

According to the CDC’s National Poison Data System, kratom-related exposure reports increased by 1,200% from 2015 to 2025 — reaching a record 3,434 reports in 2025 alone. What Every Parent Needs to Know About Kratom

Based on FDA, CDC, and state health data, here are the facts about this unregulated opioid sold in gas stations.

📦 What Is It?

What is this product? Kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) is a tropical tree leaf that contains mitragynine, an alkaloid that acts on mu-opioid receptors — same as morphine and oxycodone.

What is it sold as? Sold as “herbal supplement,” “plant-based wellness,” “energy shot,” “kratom powder,” capsules, gummies, shots, seltzers, and tea.

Is it a drug, supplement, vape, or something else? It is an unregulated psychoactive substance. The FDA says it is NOT a lawful dietary supplement or food additive. It is a drug with opioid effects.

Street names and brand names: “Kratom,” “Mit,” “Herbal Speedball,” “Ketum,” “Thom.” Brand names: Happy Hippo, Feel Free (kratom shot), Mitra-9, OPMS, Super Speciosa, 1836 Kratom, Kratom Cummies, FUZZY.

📍 Where Do Kids Get It?

Can it be bought at gas stations? Yes. Widespread in gas stations, often at checkout counters next to candy and energy drinks.

Is it sold in vape shops? Yes. Vape shops, smoke shops, and CBD stores are primary retail locations.

Can it be purchased online? Yes, easily from dozens of websites with no age verification; shipped discreetly.

Is there an age restriction? Some states have 18+ or 21+ laws, but many retailers do not check ID. No federal age restriction. Products often have small “21+” fine print on the back, ignored in practice.

🧠 Why Are Teens Using It?

What effects are they looking for? Euphoria, energy, focus, relaxation, pain relief, or a “legal high.”

Is it being marketed as energy, relaxation, focus, pain relief, or a legal high? Yes — ads claim “Calm. Focus. Energy.” “Feel good,” “microgreen,” “plant-based wellness.” Social media influencers promote “kratom energy shots” for studying or gaming.

What are people saying about it on social media? Reddit has 52,000 members in “Quitting Kratom” subreddit. TikTok/Instagram show “kratom cocktails” and “product hauls.” Teens share that it gives a “mild opioid buzz” and is easy to buy.

⚡ What Does It Do?

How does it affect the brain and body? Mitragynine binds to mu-opioid receptors, causing sedation, pain relief, euphoria at high doses; stimulant-like effects at low doses (alertness, sociability).

How quickly does it work? Within 15–30 minutes when consumed as liquid shot or tea; 30–60 minutes for capsules/powder.

How long do the effects last? 2 to 6 hours, depending on dose and form.

Does it act like an opioid, stimulant, sedative, hallucinogen, or something else? Primarily an opioid (mu-receptor agonist) with additional stimulant effects at low doses. It is not a hallucinogen.

⚠️ What Are the Risks?

Can it cause addiction? Yes. The FDA reports kratom-related substance use disorder (SUD) including tolerance, cravings, and withdrawal. Ohio’s Board of Pharmacy cites case reports of severe dependence.

Can it cause overdose? Yes. Respiratory depression can occur, especially with high-potency extracts or mixing with other depressants. Naloxone may work but not always.

Can it cause seizures? Yes. Seizures are a known adverse event from kratom use, per FDA and poison center data.

Can it cause psychosis or hallucinations? Rare, but high doses have been linked to agitation, confusion, and psychotic-like symptoms.

Can it cause heart problems? Yes. Kratom can prolong QTc interval, leading to torsades de pointes arrhythmia and sudden cardiac death. Mixing with certain medications increases risk.

Can it cause liver injury? Yes. The World Health Organization has documented at least 92 cases of liver toxicity from kratom, including jaundice and acute liver failure.

Has it been linked to deaths? Yes. CDC: 233 kratom-associated deaths (2015-2025). Ohio: 202 deaths (2019-2024). Most involve polysubstance, but single-substance fatal overdoses have occurred.

⚖️ Is It Legal?

Is it legal federally? Kratom is not federally controlled, but the FDA has declared it unlawful as a dietary supplement or food additive. The FDA has recommended Schedule I for 7‑OH (a semi-synthetic derivative), but not for whole kratom leaf.

Is it legal in my state? Banned in Alabama, Arkansas, Indiana, Rhode Island, Vermont, Wisconsin. Banned or restricted in many others. Ohio is moving to Schedule I for mitragynine (as of 2026 proposed rule). Connecticut and Utah also pending bans. Check your state.

Are there age restrictions? Some states have 18+ or 21+, but no federal law. Many retailers ignore it.

Has the FDA issued warnings? Yes. Repeated FDA warnings about liver toxicity, seizures, addiction, and death. FDA has issued warning letters to companies selling 7‑OH and kratom with false health claims. FDA also warned about heavy metal contamination (lead, nickel) and Salmonella outbreaks.

Recognize kratom packaging that appeals to children

🍬 Red flags: child-friendly design

  • Gummy shapes, fruit flavors, bright neon colors
  • Names like “Happy Hippo,” “Cummies,” “FUZZY,” “Feel Free”
  • Cartoon animals, emojis, “microgreen” health claims
  • No child-resistant packaging – bottles pop open easily
  • Placed next to real candy, energy drinks, and juice shots at checkout
  • Marketing on TikTok/Instagram using “#kratomenergy” with dance trends

🛡️ How parents can protect kids

  • Check backpacks for small colorful bottles, pouches, or gummy packs
  • Talk to teens: “Kratom is an unregulated opioid – it’s not a supplement.”
  • Report stores that sell kratom near candy to local health departments
  • Advocate for state bans (Ohio, Utah, Connecticut moving to Schedule I)
  • Use FDA MedWatch to report any kratom product that mimics candy

Separate warning: 7‑OH (7‑Hydroxymitragynine) is NOT kratom leaf

⚠️ 7‑OH is a semi‑synthetic opioid

  • Up to 13x more potent than morphine – found in “enhanced” gummies, tablets, shots
  • Marketed as “kratom” but chemically altered; FDA has called it the next wave of the opioid crisis
  • No human safety data; linked to rapid respiratory depression and death
  • Often sold in single-serving blister packs that look like candy or gum

🚨 What to do about 7‑OH

  • FDA has issued warning letters and recommended Schedule I for 7‑OH
  • Ohio, Connecticut, and Utah have already banned or are banning 7‑OH products
  • If you see “7‑OH,” “7‑Hydroxymitragynine,” “Pseudoindoxyl” on a label – treat as an emergency
  • Call poison control immediately if ingested by a child: 1-800-222-1222
