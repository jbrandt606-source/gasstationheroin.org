Kratom: a hidden opioid crisis inside gas stations and vape shops.

Kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) is promoted as a natural mood booster, but concentrated extracts and “enhanced” products like 7‑hydroxymitragynine (7‑OH) cause severe dependence, respiratory depression, seizures, and death. Unregulated, mislabeled, and aggressively marketed to young adults through social media and colorful packaging.

Recognize kratom packaging & deceptive marketing

🧃 Red flags on shelves

  • Names like “Feel Free,” “Happy Hippo,” “Mitra-9,” “Kratom Cummies,” “FUZZY”
  • Labels: “plant‑based,” “herbal supplement,” “energy shot” — no opioid warning
  • “7‑OH,” “ultra enhanced,” “black,” “pseudoindoxyl” – code for high potency extracts
  • Packaging mimicking energy drinks, candy, or wellness products
  • Sold next to vapes, pre‑rolls, delta‑8, and tobacco

🛡️ How you can fight back

  • Report suspicious kratom shots / gummies to FDA MedWatch
  • Call out local gas stations & smoke shops that sell “Feel Free” or high‑concentration extracts
  • Advocate for state bans on 7‑OH and unlabeled kratom extracts
  • Share this gallery with parent groups, PTAs, and youth coalition meetings
  • Learn withdrawal signs: agitation, insomnia, runny nose, muscle pain, cravings

What parents need to know about kratom

📦 Common product forms

  • Powders & capsules (“Maeng Da”, “Bali”, “Sumatra”)
  • Liquid shots (Jubi, MIT45, OPMS Black) – extremely concentrated
  • Gummies, chewables, tablets (often fruit flavors)
  • Kratom seltzers / “feel‑good” drinks (Mitra-9, Feel Free)

🚨 Signs of teen kratom use

  • Small colorful bottles / opaque pouches in backpack
  • Unexplained drowsiness, nausea, constipation
  • Withdrawal: anxiety, sweating, tremors, cravings
  • Spending money at vape shops / gas stations frequently
