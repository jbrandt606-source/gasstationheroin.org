The hidden dangers on the shelf

Gas stations and smoke shops across America sell dangerous, unregulated opioid-like compounds. Below are the most common substances you need to know about.

KRATOM

Mitragynine and 7-OH are partial opioid agonists. Widely sold as powder, capsules, shots. Can cause seizures, liver toxicity, and dependence. Often mixed with other synthetics.

Highly variable potency

7‑OH (7-Hydroxymitragynine)

Up to 13x more potent than morphine. Found in “enhanced” kratom products. Rapid addiction, respiratory depression, overdose risk. Sold as chewable tablets, gummies.

Extreme potency

MGM‑15

Synthetic opioid receptor agonist, sold in “research chemical” blends. No human safety data. Linked to severe respiratory depression.

Experimental

Pseudoindoxyl

Potent semi-synthetic kratom alkaloid, up to 20x stronger than morphine. Found in “Ultra Seven” and “Pseudo” shots. High addiction liability.

Extreme potency

Tianeptine

Known as “gas station heroin.” Tricyclic antidepressant that acts as a full mu‑opioid agonist at high doses. Causes severe withdrawal, seizures, deaths.

Gas station heroin

Akuamma

African seed traditionally used for pain, but now sold as a “natural opioid.” Can interact dangerously with other depressants.

Understudied

A‑238

Novel synthetic opioid, structurally related to U‑class drugs. No medical use, sold as “research chemical.” Extreme overdose risk.

Deadly potent

U‑class chemicals (U-47700, U-48800)

“Pink” or “U‑4” – illicit fentanyl analogs found in gas station “incense.” Numerous fatalities reported.

DEA Schedule I

SR‑17018

Novel opioid with biased agonism, sold online and in some vape shops as “legal high.” No safety data, linked to coma and respiratory failure.

Extreme danger

Recognize the packaging & take action

Signs to watch for

  • “Not for human consumption” / “Research chemical”
  • Small plastic jars or colorful vials with gas station labels
  • Names like “Za Za,” “Tianaa,” “Pegasus,” “Neptune’s Fix” (tianeptine)
  • “7-OH,” “Pseudo,” “Ultra” blends
  • QR codes that lead to dosing instructions

What you can do

  • Report suspicious products to the FDA via MedWatch
  • Contact local health departments and law enforcement
  • Advocate for state bans on tianeptine and unlabeled synthetic opioids
  • Share this guide with schools and parent groups
