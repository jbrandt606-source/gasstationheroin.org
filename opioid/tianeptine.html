What Every Parent Must Know About Tianeptine

Based on FDA warnings, CDC poison center data, and state health records. Tianeptine is not a supplement. It is an antidepressant that becomes a dangerous opioid when abused.

What Is It?

What is this product? Tianeptine is a tricyclic antidepressant. At low therapeutic doses (12.5mg), it has mild effects. But the products sold in gas stations contain 150mg, 200mg, or even 300mg per dose. At these high doses, tianeptine acts as a full mu-opioid receptor agonist — exactly like heroin or morphine. It is often called "gas station heroin" for a reason.

What is it sold as? Sold as “dietary supplement,” “elixir,” “capsules,” “tablets,” “energy shot.” Brand names: Za Za (Red, Silver), Tianaa (Red, White, Green), Neptune's Fix, Neptune's Elixir, Wide Awake, Tropicat Elixir, TD Red.

Is it a drug, supplement, vape, or something else? It is an unapproved, illegal drug when sold for human consumption in the U.S. The FDA has declared tianeptine products adulterated and has issued multiple warning letters. It is not a dietary supplement by any legal definition.

Street names and brand names: “Gas station heroin,” “Za Za,” “Tianaa,” “Neptune’s Fix,” “Neptune’s Elixir,” “Wide Awake,” “Tropicat,” “TD Red.”

Where Do Kids Get It?

Can it be bought at gas stations? Yes. Widely available at gas stations, often next to the cash register alongside energy drinks and candy.

Is it sold in vape shops? Yes. Vape shops, smoke shops, and CBD stores are primary retail locations. Often sold in shiny bottles that look like energy shots.

Can it be purchased online? Yes. Numerous websites sell tianeptine with no age verification. Shipped in discreet packaging.

Is there an age restriction? No federal law. Some states have banned tianeptine, but in states where it's still legal, there is no age verification. Bottles say “21+” in fine print, universally ignored.

Why Are Teens Using It?

Is it being marketed as energy, relaxation, focus, pain relief, or a legal high? Marketing uses “happiness in a bottle,” “elixir,” “wide awake,” “extra strength.” Some products claim to be for “focus” or “mood support.” These are lies to hide the opioid effect.

What are people saying about it on social media? Reddit communities like r/QuittingTianeptine and r/Tianeptine show thousands of users addicted, suffering brutal withdrawal, and trying to quit. Many report spending hundreds of dollars per day on gas station tianeptine products. Some compare the withdrawal to heroin or fentanyl.

What Does It Do?

How does it affect the brain and body? At high doses, tianeptine is a full mu-opioid agonist. It produces euphoria, pain relief, sedation, respiratory depression, constipation, and pinpoint pupils. It also affects serotonin and dopamine, adding stimulant-like effects at low doses.

How quickly does it work? Liquid elixirs work in 10–20 minutes. Capsules take 30–60 minutes.

How long do the effects last? 2–4 hours, but tolerance builds rapidly. Users quickly need higher doses to achieve the same effect.

Does it act like an opioid, stimulant, sedative, hallucinogen, or something else? It is primarily an opioid at abused doses. Some users report mild stimulant effects at low doses, but the dangerous effects are opioid-based.

What Are the Risks?

Can it cause addiction? Yes. Extremely addictive. Users report severe withdrawal within hours of last dose after only a few weeks of daily use. Withdrawal includes vomiting, diarrhea, severe anxiety, muscle pain, insomnia, and intense cravings.

Can it cause overdose? Yes. Respiratory depression can be fatal. Because gas station products vary wildly in potency, users easily take too much. Overdose looks like heroin overdose — unresponsive, blue lips, shallow breathing.

Can it cause seizures? Yes. Tianeptine is known to lower seizure threshold. Many poison center reports of seizures after high-dose tianeptine use.

Can it cause psychosis or hallucinations? Yes. High doses can cause confusion, agitation, and psychotic episodes.

Can it cause heart problems? Yes. Tianeptine can cause tachycardia, hypertension, arrhythmias, and in rare cases, cardiac arrest.

Can it cause liver injury? Yes. Several case reports of acute liver failure requiring transplant after tianeptine abuse.

Has it been linked to deaths? Yes. The CDC and FDA confirm multiple tianeptine-associated deaths. Poison center calls for tianeptine increased over 350% in recent years. Many overdoses involve tianeptine alone.

Is It Legal?

Is it legal federally? Tianeptine is not FDA-approved for any use in the U.S. It is not a lawful dietary supplement. The FDA has issued warning letters and import alerts, but it remains sold in a gray market.

Is it legal in my state? Multiple states have banned tianeptine: Alabama, Arkansas, Georgia, Indiana, Kentucky, Michigan, Mississippi, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Virginia, West Virginia, and others. In states without bans, it is still sold openly. Check local laws.

Are there age restrictions? No federal. Some states have 18+ or 21+, but rarely enforced.

Has the FDA issued warnings? Yes. The FDA has repeatedly warned consumers about tianeptine, calling it a dangerous opioid that has led to hospitalizations, overdoses, and deaths. The agency has sent warning letters to manufacturers and issued import alerts.
