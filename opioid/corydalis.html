Corydalis: From herbal sedative to gas station opioid booster.

Corydalis yanhusuo contains tetrahydropalmatine (THP) and other alkaloids that act on dopamine receptors (D1/D2 antagonists) and, at higher doses, may have opioid-like effects via the 13-OH metabolite. Traditionally used for menstrual pain and as a sedative, it is now being extracted, concentrated, and blended with mitragynine (kratom's main alkaloid) in products sold as "proprietary euphoric blends." These combinations have never been studied in humans. No one knows how corydalis extracts interact with mu-opioid agonists like mitragynine. The result is an unregulated pharmacological experiment.

What You Need to Know About Corydalis Extracts & Blends

Corydalis is appearing as an ingredient in "enhanced" kratom products and standalone extracts. The concern is not the herb itself, but the concentrated forms and unpredictable combinations.

What Is Corydalis?

What is this product? Corydalis refers to the tuber of Corydalis yanhusuo, a plant used in traditional Chinese medicine. Its primary alkaloid, tetrahydropalmatine (THP), has sedative, analgesic, and dopamine-blocking effects. A related compound, 13-OH (13-hydroxycorydaline), is being studied for opioid receptor activity.

What is it sold as? Whole herb powder, capsules, liquid extracts, and as an ingredient in "proprietary blends" with kratom/mitragynine. Labels often say "Corydalis extract," "THP," "13-OH," or "proprietary euphoric blend."

Is it a drug, supplement, or something else? It is marketed as a dietary supplement, but concentrated extracts and blends may be considered unapproved new drugs. The FDA has not evaluated safety or efficacy.

What are the active compounds? Tetrahydropalmatine (THP) is the best known – it blocks dopamine D1/D2 receptors and binds to the GABA system. 13-OH is a related alkaloid that may have mu-opioid agonist properties. Neither has been approved for human use in concentrated form.

Why Are People Using It?

What effects are they looking for? Sedation, relaxation, pain relief, mood elevation, and as a "potentiator" for kratom. Some users report that corydalis enhances the euphoric effects of mitragynine or reduces opioid tolerance.

What are "proprietary euphoric blends"? These are undisclosed mixtures of corydalis extract, mitragynine, and sometimes other botanicals (akuamma, kava, etc.). Sellers claim "synergistic" effects without providing any evidence. The term "proprietary" masks exact ingredients and ratios.

What are people saying online? Forums like Reddit discuss "corydalis + kratom" as a way to get a stronger high or to manage withdrawal. Some report dangerous sedation, difficulty waking up, and intense nausea. There is no medical guidance.

What Are the Concerns About Blends?

Why are combinations dangerous? Corydalis alkaloids can cause respiratory depression when combined with mu-opioid agonists (like mitragynine). THP also causes dopamine blockade, which can lead to Parkinson-like symptoms, hormone changes, and sedation. The interactions are completely unstudied.

Has any research been done? Very little. Most studies on corydalis are isolated alkaloid studies in animals. No human trials have examined concentrated extracts or combinations with kratom. There are no established safe doses, no LD50 data, and no long-term safety studies.

What about "13-OH"? 13-OH is a semisynthetic derivative being marketed as a "cleaner" alternative. It has no human safety data. Preliminary reports suggest it may have higher affinity for mu-opioid receptors than THP, raising overdose risk.

What Does It Do to the Body?

How does it affect the brain? THP is primarily a dopamine antagonist – it blocks the reward pathway, which is the opposite of typical opioids. This can cause sedation, anhedonia (lack of pleasure), and movement disorders. 13-OH may act as an opioid agonist. The net effect of a blend is unpredictable.

How quickly does it work? Extracts and blends take 30–60 minutes to take effect, depending on formulation.

How long do effects last? 2–6 hours. THP has a long half-life (over 6 hours), so repeated daily use can lead to accumulation.

Can it cause opioid-like effects? The combination with mitragynine can cause opioid effects: euphoria, respiratory depression, constipation. Pure corydalis extract is more sedating and dysphoric.

What Are the Known Risks?

Can it cause addiction? THP itself is not considered addictive because it blocks dopamine. However, the euphoric blends with mitragynine are addictive due to the opioid component. Withdrawal from such blends may include both opioid and dopamine-related symptoms.

Can it cause overdose? Yes. Respiratory depression is possible when corydalis is combined with mu-opioid agonists. Overdose may not respond fully to naloxone if THP contributes to sedation.

Can it cause movement disorders? Dopamine antagonists like THP can cause parkinsonism (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia) and tardive dyskinesia with long-term use.

Can it cause liver injury? Unknown. No studies. Botanical extracts often contain hepatotoxic contaminants.

Has it been linked to deaths? No direct deaths solely from corydalis, but blends with kratom or other opioids may contribute to polysubstance fatalities. Toxicological screens often don't test for corydalis alkaloids.

Is It Legal?

Is it legal federally? Yes, currently. Corydalis extracts are not scheduled. However, when marketed for euphoria or as a drug, they may violate FDA regulations against unapproved new drugs.

Are there state restrictions? None specific to corydalis. However, some states have "analog" laws that could apply to 13-OH if it is structurally similar to a controlled substance.

Has the FDA taken action? Not yet, but as "proprietary euphoric blends" proliferate, warning letters are likely. The FDA has previously targeted brands selling kratom-corydalis combinations for unproven claims.

The emerging threat: Corydalis extracts, especially 13-OH, are being added to gas station opioid products as "potentiators." Products like "UFORIA 13-HYDROXY" directly combine mitragynine with 13-OH – a completely unstudied mixture. Others like "ketaMIND" market "euphoric" effects without any safety data. These blends are not traditional herbal remedies; they are designer drug combinations. Until regulatory action is taken, consumers are guinea pigs in a dangerous experiment.

What Can Be Done About Unregulated Corydalis Blends?

Red flags in the marketplace

  • Claims of "euphoric," "proprietary blend," "synergistic" – code for unstudied drug combinations
  • Products that directly combine corydalis with kratom/mitragynine extracts (e.g., UFORIA 13-HYDROXY)
  • No warning labels about dopamine antagonism or respiratory depression
  • Concentrated extracts with no dosage guidelines
  • Marketing to people seeking "legal highs" or opioid alternatives

How to address the emerging threat

  • Educate consumers: "Corydalis extracts with mitragynine are unstudied drug combinations."
  • Report adverse events (sedation, breathing difficulty, movement disorders) to FDA MedWatch.
  • Advocate for state regulation of "proprietary euphoric blends" under analog acts.
  • Support research into the pharmacology of 13-OH and THP-opioid interactions.
  • File a complaint with the FTC about deceptive marketing of unproven "blends": reportfraud.ftc.gov
