What Is It?

What is this product? 7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) is a semi-synthetic opioid alkaloid. It is chemically altered from kratom leaf to maximize mu-opioid receptor activation. It is NOT natural.

What is it sold as? Sold as “chewable tablets,” “gummies,” “shots,” “stacks,” “tabs.” Flavors: Blue Raspberry, Lemon, Strawberry, Cookies & Cream, Watermelon Fruit Punch, Orange Mint.

Is it a drug, supplement, vape, or something else? It is an unapproved, illegal drug when sold for human consumption. The FDA has declared 7-OH products adulterated and has issued warning letters. It has no accepted medical use.

Street names and brand names: “7-OH,” “7-Hydroxy,” “Hydroxie,” “7OH Plus,” “Ultra Seven,” “Stacks,” “Kures,” “Pure7,” “Method 7-OH,” “Real Botanicals 7-Hydro,” “7OF,” “HEAT.”

Where Do Kids Get It?

Can it be bought at gas stations? Yes. Same gas station checkout aisles as candy, energy drinks, and vapes. Sold openly.

Is it sold in vape shops? Yes, prominently displayed alongside delta-8 and kratom. Often called “kratom alkaloid” to hide the danger.

Can it be purchased online? Yes. Dozens of websites ship 7-OH products with no age verification. Ads on social media target teens with “level up,” “big savings,” “back in stock.”

Is there an age restriction? None federally. Some states are banning it, but most retailers ignore ID checks. Packaging says “21+” in tiny fine print — never enforced.

Why Are Teens Using It?

What effects are they looking for? A strong, rapid opioid high — euphoria, sedation, “nodding off.” Because it’s cheap, legal, and easy to get.

Is it being marketed as energy, relaxation, focus, pain relief, or a legal high? Ads use “confidence starts with control,” “big savings,” “unbeatable prices,” “premium botanical extracts.” They hide the opioid truth behind “wellness” language.

What Does It Do?

How does it affect the brain and body? 7-OH is a full mu-opioid receptor agonist. It produces intense euphoria, pain relief, sedation, respiratory depression, and constipation. At higher doses, it causes coma and death.

How quickly does it work? Chewable tablets and sublingual shots work in 5–15 minutes. Faster than morphine.

How long do the effects last? 3–6 hours, but withdrawal begins rapidly after repeated use.

Does it act like an opioid, stimulant, sedative, hallucinogen, or something else? It is a potent opioid. No stimulant effects at typical doses. Overdose looks exactly like heroin or fentanyl overdose — pinpoint pupils, respiratory arrest.

What Are the Risks?

Can it cause addiction? Absolutely. One week of daily use can cause severe physical dependence. Withdrawal includes vomiting, diarrhea, muscle cramps, anxiety, insomnia — worse than many prescription opioids.

Can it cause overdose? Yes. Because potency varies wildly (10mg to 150mg per tablet), users easily take too much. Respiratory depression kills.

Can it cause seizures? Yes. Numerous poison center reports of seizures after 7-OH ingestion, especially in combination with other drugs.

Can it cause psychosis or hallucinations? High doses can cause delirium, agitation, and psychotic episodes.

Can it cause heart problems? Yes. QTc prolongation, arrhythmias, cardiac arrest — documented in case reports.

Can it cause liver injury? Emerging evidence of hepatotoxicity similar to kratom but more severe due to higher potency.

Has it been linked to deaths? Yes. The CDC and FDA confirm multiple single-substance 7-OH fatalities. Postmortem toxicology shows lethal blood concentrations of 7-OH alone.

Is It Legal?

Is it legal federally? Not for human consumption. The FDA has issued import alerts and warning letters. In July 2025, FDA recommended Schedule I for 7-OH. But it remains sold as “research chemical” or “kratom alkaloid” in a gray market.

Is it legal in my state? Ohio banned 7-OH in December 2025 (emergency rule). Connecticut and Utah have moved to ban. Most states have no law — yet. Check local regulations.

Are there age restrictions? No federal. Some states 18+ or 21+ but rarely enforced.

Has the FDA issued warnings? Yes. FDA Commissioner Dr. Martin Makary warned that 7-OH “could be the next wave of the opioid crisis.” Multiple FDA warning letters to manufacturers for illegal marketing.
