Phenibut
Unregulated Sedative

A powerful, unregulated sedative sold in gas stations, vape shops, and online — often disguised as sleep aids, focus supplements, or anti‑stress formulas.

Phenibut is not a dietary supplement. It is a psychoactive drug sold next to energy drinks and candy.

Phenibut is a central nervous system depressant developed in Russia for anxiety and insomnia. Today, it is sold in the U.S. as “Relax-All,” “Red Dawn,” “Sleep Walk,” “Social EazzZ,” and dozens of other gas‑station brands. It is often labeled as a “dietary supplement” — but it is not approved for any use by the FDA.

You can walk into a convenience store, vape shop, or truck stop and buy phenibut in capsules, powders, shots, or tablet form. It promises relaxation, sleep, focus, or “extra energy” — but the withdrawal and addiction potential is real.

It looks like a wellness product. Capsules, powders, and shots are sold in sleek packaging with words like “Relax-All,” “Tranquil,” and “Calmness to Muscles.”
It is sold openly everywhere. Gas stations, smoke shops, vape stores, head shops, truck stops, and hundreds of websites.
It hides behind vague claims. “Stress and anxiety relief,” “supports restful sleep,” “feelings of well-being” — never warns about dependence.
Withdrawal can be severe. Users report anxiety, insomnia, hallucinations, paranoia, and even seizures after stopping regular use.

Phenibut, explained in plain English

No medical degree needed. This is an unregulated, addictive sedative being sold in ordinary stores as if it were a vitamin or a relaxation aid.

What is phenibut?

Phenibut (β-phenyl-γ-aminobutyric acid) is a synthetic drug that acts on GABA receptors in the brain — similar to benzodiazepines or alcohol. It was developed in the 1960s for anxiety and sleep disorders in Russia, but it is not FDA-approved for any medical use in the United States.

Despite that, it is sold as a “dietary supplement” in gas stations, vape shops, and online. The packaging often calls it “Relax-All,” “Red Dawn,” “Sleep Walk,” “Tranquil Body & Mind,” or other friendly names.

Why should parents and communities care?

Because phenibut is sold beside energy drinks, vapes, and candy. It looks harmless. A tired driver might buy “Extra Energy!” Red Dawn. A student might try “Focus Increase” capsules. An anxious person might buy “Stress & Anxiety Relief” shots.

  • It creates a false sense of safety — “if it’s sold here, it must be okay.”
  • It is often unlabeled or mislabeled, leading to accidental overdose.
  • Teens and young adults can buy it without age restriction in many states.
  • People in recovery may not realize it acts on the same pathways as benzodiazepines.

Where is phenibut sold?

Gas stations, convenience stores, vape shops, smoke shops, CBD stores, truck stops, head shops, and countless online retailers. It is often placed near kratom, kava, energy shots, or CBD products.

Some brands use vending machines, display racks near the register, or “impulse buy” shelves right next to gum and candy bars.

How is it marketed to look safe?

The marketing uses calming, natural-sounding language that hides the risks:

  • “Supports Relaxation & Calmness to Muscles”
  • “Supports Restful Sleep”
  • “Supports Feelings of Well-Being”
  • “Anti Stress & Anxiety”
  • “Tranquil Body & Mind”
  • “Extra Energy!” — even though it is a sedative

None of these labels warn about dependence, withdrawal, or the danger of mixing with alcohol or other sedatives.

What does phenibut do to the body?

Phenibut produces sedation, relaxation, reduced anxiety, and at higher doses, euphoria or intoxication. It can impair coordination, memory, and judgment. Overdose can cause extreme sedation, nausea, vomiting, unresponsiveness, and slowed breathing — especially when mixed with alcohol or other drugs.

Regular use leads to tolerance: users need more to get the same effect. When they try to stop, withdrawal can be brutal — including severe anxiety, insomnia, agitation, psychosis, and seizures.

Is it legal?

Currently, phenibut is not a controlled substance at the federal level, though the FDA has issued warnings about its use and considers it an “unapproved drug.” Several states have banned or restricted it (e.g., Michigan, Ohio, Louisiana, and others). But in most of the country, it remains perfectly legal to sell in gas stations.

The legal gray area means many store owners don’t even know what they are selling — they just stock whatever a distributor offers.

Can phenibut cause addiction?

Yes. Phenibut is known for rapid tolerance and severe physical dependence. Withdrawal symptoms can last for weeks and include panic attacks, paranoia, hallucinations, tremors, and in extreme cases, seizures or delirium.

Online forums and emergency rooms have documented dozens of cases of phenibut withdrawal requiring hospitalization. This is not a mild supplement — it is a drug that can trap people without them ever intending to become dependent.

What about the “energy” phenibut products?

Some brands, like “Red Dawn” and “Extra Energy!” shots, sell phenibut as a stimulant — even though it is a sedative. This is dangerously misleading. Users expecting a caffeine-like boost may take higher doses, leading to unexpected sedation, blackouts, or accidents.

Combining phenibut with caffeine, alcohol, or other drugs is extremely unpredictable and can cause serious harm.

What to look for in your neighborhood

Red flags on shelves

  • Products with “Phenibut” on the label — often in small print under names like “Relax-All,” “Red Dawn,” “Sleep Walk,” “Social EazzZ,” “Tranquil,” or “Calmness to Muscles”
  • Capsules, shots, or powders labeled for “stress,” “anxiety,” “relaxation,” “focus,” or “energy” — especially in gas stations or vape shops
  • Packages that say “Dietary Supplement” but contain synthetic sedative compounds
  • Products with patent numbers (like #6,582,735) to sound legitimate
  • Placement near kratom, kava, CBD, energy shots, or checkout candy

What communities can do

  • Ask local gas stations and vape shops: “Do you know that phenibut is an unregulated sedative that can cause withdrawal and seizures?”
  • Warn schools, parent groups, recovery meetings, and community organizations.
  • Report misleading marketing to the FTC: reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • Contact state legislators and health departments to ban gas-station phenibut sales.
  • Take photos of products and shelf displays — documentation helps change laws.
