Kava, explained in plain English

No anthropology degree needed. This is a plant with real sedative effects that is being sold in gas stations in forms that look like candy, energy gummies, and wellness tonics — with no safety warnings or age restrictions.

What is kava?

Kava (Piper methysticum) is a plant from the South Pacific whose roots contain compounds called kavalactones. These compounds act on the central nervous system to produce relaxation, muscle relaxation, sedation, and mild euphoria. It is often consumed as a drink in cultural ceremonies.

In recent years, companies have started selling concentrated kava extracts in gummies, tonics, shots, and capsules — available in gas stations, vape shops, and online, with no prescription or age verification.

Why should parents and communities be concerned?

Because kava is being sold in products that look like candy, energy gummies, or healthy tonics — right next to the checkout counter. The marketing targets young people, stressed adults, and even college athletes.

  • Misleading forms: “Grape silly kava energy” gummies, “goomz mushroom kava sleep” gummies, “feel free” tonics — they look harmless.
  • No age restrictions: A teenager can buy kava gummies or shots without ID in most states.
  • Deceptive endorsements: Some brands claim to be the “official tonic” of FSU, Texas, USC athletics — implying safety and performance.
  • Liver toxicity risk: Kava has been linked to severe liver damage, especially when combined with alcohol or medications.

Where is kava sold?

Gas stations, vape shops, smoke shops, CBD stores, convenience stores, head shops, and hundreds of online retailers. Look for brands like “feel free,” “silly kava,” “goomz,” “Ecstasy” kava + kratom + kanna blends, and others.

Products are often placed near kratom, phenibut, blue lotus, delta-8, and other gas station drugs — sometimes in the same display case.

How is it marketed to look safe?

The marketing uses “natural,” “plant-based,” and “wellness” language to hide risks:

  • “Plant based herbal supplement”
  • “Deep sleep and relaxation”
  • “All natural mushroom gummy”
  • “Nutritionally supplemented”
  • “Official tonic of [University] athletics” — implying safety and legitimacy

Some products even combine kava with other unregulated substances like kratom, kanna, or reishi mushrooms — creating unpredictable effects.

What are the effects and risks of kava?

Kava produces relaxation, reduced anxiety, muscle relaxation, and sedation. At higher doses, users report dizziness, impaired coordination, and a “heavy” feeling. Driving or operating machinery after kava use is dangerous.

Serious risk: Kava has been associated with liver toxicity, including hepatitis, cirrhosis, and liver failure. The FDA has issued warnings about kava-containing supplements and liver damage. The risk increases with alcohol use or pre-existing liver conditions.

Is kava legal?

Kava is not a controlled substance at the federal level in the U.S., though several countries (including the UK, Canada, and some European nations) have restricted or banned it due to liver toxicity concerns. In the U.S., it is widely available with no age restrictions — you can buy kava gummies or shots at a gas station at any age.

The lack of regulation means no dosage standards, no safety warnings, and no requirement to disclose liver risks on packaging.

What about “feel free” and sports endorsements?

Some kava brands, like “feel free,” claim to be the “official tonic” of major university athletics programs. This is extremely misleading — it implies the product has been tested, approved, or is safe for athletes. In reality, kava can cause sedation, impaired reaction time, and liver problems — the opposite of what athletes need.

Parents should be especially alert if their kids see kava products endorsed by their favorite college teams.

What about kava combined with kratom, kanna, or mushrooms?

Some products (like “Ecstasy” gummies shown in our gallery) combine kava with kratom (unregulated opioid-like plant), kanna (Sceletium tortuosum, a psychoactive succulent), or reishi mushrooms. These combinations are completely unstudied and can lead to unpredictable sedation, nausea, dizziness, and impaired driving.

A product labeled “Ecstasy” alone should be a major red flag — it is marketing a drug-like experience, not a safe herbal supplement.

What to look for in your neighborhood

Red flags on shelves

  • Products labeled “Kava,” “Feel Free,” “Silly Kava,” “Goomz,” or “Ecstasy” (especially if also containing kratom/kanna)
  • Gummies, tonics, shots, or capsules claiming “relaxation,” “sleep,” “energy,” or “plant based”
  • Products that combine kava with kratom, kanna, mushrooms, or other unregulated herbs
  • Marketing that says “official tonic of [college athletics]” — this is a deceptive tactic
  • Placement near kratom, phenibut, delta-8, or checkout counters in gas stations and vape shops
  • No warning labels about liver toxicity or not mixing with alcohol

What communities can do

  • Ask local gas stations and vape shops: “Do you know kava has been linked to liver damage and the FDA has issued warnings?”
  • Warn schools, parent groups, athletic departments, and community organizations about kava gummies and tonics.
  • Report deceptive marketing (like “official tonic of athletics” without safety evidence) to the FTC: reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • Take photos of products and shelf displays — documentation helps local news and lawmakers act.
  • Contact state legislators and health departments to restrict gas-station sales of unregulated sedatives.
