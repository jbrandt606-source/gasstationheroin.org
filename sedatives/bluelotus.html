Blue lotus, explained in plain English

No botanical knowledge required. This is a plant with psychoactive effects that is being sold in gas stations in forms that look like candy and vapes — with no warnings, age checks, or safety standards.

What is blue lotus?

Blue lotus (Nymphaea caerulea) is a water lily historically used in Egyptian rituals for its mild calming and euphoric effects. It contains compounds like apomorphine and nuciferine, which can act on dopamine and serotonin receptors.

In recent years, companies have started selling concentrated extracts in gas stations and vape shops — not as a tea or ceremonial herb, but as high-dose gummies, disposable vapes, and “infused” edibles marketed to a young, recreational audience.

Why should parents and communities be concerned?

Because blue lotus products are being sold in the same places as candy, soda, and vapes. The packaging is often colorful, youthful, and enticing.

  • Misleading forms: Gummies labeled “Stoned,” “Blueberry,” “18+” — they look like regular candy but contain psychoactive plant extracts.
  • No age restrictions in many states: A teenager can walk in and buy a disposable blue lotus vape or a 6000mg bag of gummies.
  • Deceptive wellness claims: “Plant medicine,” “100% profit to charity,” “sleep, good dreams” — none of this guarantees safety.
  • Unknown drug interactions: Blue lotus can cause sedation, dizziness, confusion, and impaired driving — especially when mixed with alcohol or other drugs.

Where is blue lotus sold?

Gas stations, vape shops, smoke shops, CBD stores, head shops, convenience stores, and hundreds of online retailers. You will see brands like “Stoned,” “Blue Lotus,” “Super Goji,” “Lotus Kush,” “Psyched,” “Jolly Green Oil,” and “CannaStick.”

Products can be found near the register, on spinning racks, or next to kratom, delta-8, and other gas station drugs.

How is it marketed to look safe?

The marketing uses “natural,” “ancient,” and “wellness” language to hide risks:

  • “Plant Medicine”
  • “Sleep, Good Dreams”
  • “Vegan, Gluten Free”
  • “100% Profit to Charity”
  • “Blue Lotus” — sounds exotic and harmless

Some brands even use the word “Stoned” openly, targeting recreational users without disclosing side effects, withdrawal, or overdose potential.

What are the effects and risks?

Blue lotus can produce mild euphoria, relaxation, dream-like states, and sedation. At higher doses (especially in concentrated extracts), users report dizziness, confusion, nausea, and significant drowsiness. Driving or operating machinery after use is dangerous.

Because there is no federal regulation, actual potency varies wildly — some gummies claim 500mg, 1000mg, or even 6000mg per package. Consumers have no way to know what they are actually ingesting.

Is it legal?

Blue lotus is not a controlled substance at the federal level in the U.S. However, several states (like Louisiana, Indiana, and others) have banned or restricted it. It is also illegal in some European countries. But in most of America, it remains completely unregulated — you can buy it in a gas station at any age.

The lack of regulation means no dosage standards, no safety warnings, no child-proof packaging, and no testing for contaminants.

Are the vapes especially dangerous?

Yes. Disposable blue lotus vapes (like “Stoned Blue Lotus Boomg Vape” or “Super Goji” pens) deliver an unknown amount of concentrated extract directly to the lungs. The long-term effects of vaping blue lotus are completely unknown. Teens may mistake them for nicotine or CBD vapes.

The vape products often use bright colors, candy flavors like strawberry, and sleek hardware that appeals to young people.

What about “Lotus Kush” or “Psyched” products?

Names like “Lotus Kush” mimic cannabis branding. “Psyched” implies a psychedelic experience. These are marketing tactics to attract people looking for a legal high. But the actual effects, safety, and ingredients are not reviewed by any health authority.

Some products combine blue lotus with other unregulated herbs (kratom, kava, phenibut, or synthetic cannabinoids) — increasing the risk of dangerous interactions.

What to look for in your neighborhood

Red flags on shelves

  • Products labeled “Blue Lotus,” “Stoned,” “Lotus Kush,” “Psyched,” “Super Goji,” or “Jolly Green Oil”
  • Gummies in bright packaging with fruit flavors (blueberry, strawberry) — often with “18+” in small print
  • Disposable vape pens or cartridges claiming “blue lotus” or “plant medicine”
  • Packages with dosage claims like 500mg, 1000mg, or 6000mg — no regulation means those numbers are not verified
  • Products sold near kratom, delta-8, candy, energy drinks, or checkout counters in gas stations and vape shops
  • Wellness language like “sleep, good dreams,” “100% profit to charity,” “vegan, gluten free” — used to distract from psychoactive effects

What communities can do

  • Ask local gas stations and vape shops: “Do you know blue lotus can cause sedation and is not regulated for safety?”
  • Warn schools, parent groups, sports leagues, and community organizations about blue lotus gummies and vapes.
  • Take photos of products and shelf displays — documentation helps local news and lawmakers act.
  • Report misleading or deceptive marketing to the FTC: reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • Contact state legislators and local health departments to restrict gas-station sales of psychoactive botanicals.
