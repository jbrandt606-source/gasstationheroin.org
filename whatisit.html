What is Kratom? What is 7-OH?

What is Kratom?

Kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) is a tropical plant from Southeast Asia that contains psychoactive alkaloids — especially mitragynine. In the U.S., it’s sold unregulated in vape shops, gas stations, and online as a powder, capsule, tea, or “energy” shot. Mitragynine acts on the brain’s opioid receptors, producing stimulant or sedative effects depending on the dose. Kratom use can cause dependence, withdrawal, and overdose-like toxicity. It is not approved for any medical use by the FDA and has been linked to seizures, liver damage, and death.

What is 7-OH?

7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) is a metabolic byproduct of mitragynine — and also found in small amounts in some kratom products. It is far more potent at opioid receptors than morphine. 7-OH is responsible for most of kratom’s narcotic-like effects. Some manufacturers spike products with synthetic 7-OH and label it as a “premium” or “enhanced” formulation. 7-OH is directly associated with respiratory depression, overdose, and hospitalizations requiring naloxone reversal.

Why a 7-OH Ban Isn’t Enough

While 7-OH is the most potent opioid in kratom, most serious health effects — including fatalities — are caused by mitragynine itself. Mitragynine converts to 7-OH in the liver, especially when users concentrate kratom by boiling it into a tea or reducing it into a resin. The majority of kratom-related deaths involve unmodified kratom powders and capsules, not products that were labeled “7-OH.” If legislation only targets 7-OH, the core public health risk remains on store shelves.
