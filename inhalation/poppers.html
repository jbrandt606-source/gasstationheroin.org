What are poppers?

Poppers are a class of chemicals called alkyl nitrites (e.g., amyl nitrite, isobutyl nitrite, isopropyl nitrite). They are inhaled for a rapid, short-lived “rush” — a feeling of warmth, dizziness, euphoria, and relaxation of smooth muscles, including the anal and vaginal sphincters.

To avoid drug laws, poppers are often sold in small bottles labeled as “leather cleaner,” “room odorizer,” “tape head cleaner,” “liquid incense,” or “nail polish remover.” Common brand names include Jungle Juice, Rush, Super Rush, Hell Fire, Speed, and Ecstasy Pop. They are legal in many states (not intended for human consumption), but inhaling them carries real health risks.

Rapid onset, short duration Effects begin within seconds and last 1–3 minutes. Users may inhale repeatedly.
Misleading labeling Sold as “solvent cleaner,” “room deodorizer,” or “leather cleaner” to circumvent drug regulations.
Common in certain settings Often used in club, rave, and LGBTQ+ party scenes, but also sold in gas stations and vape shops.
Not safe to swallow Poppers are volatile liquids meant for inhalation only. Swallowing can be fatal.

What families and community members should know

Plain-English answers about poppers — the products, how they are used, and the risks.

What are poppers chemically?

Poppers contain alkyl nitrites — most commonly amyl nitrite, isobutyl nitrite, or isopropyl nitrite. Amyl nitrite was once prescribed for angina (chest pain), but prescription use has largely been replaced. Modern “poppers” are sold as industrial or household products to avoid drug laws.

How are they used?

The liquid is poured into a small bottle or applied to a cloth or cotton ball. Users inhale the vapor through the nose or mouth. The effects are immediate and last 1–3 minutes. Some people inhale repeatedly during a session.

What effects do they produce?

Users report a warm “rush,” dizziness, lightheadedness, euphoria, relaxation, and reduced inhibitions. Poppers also relax smooth muscles, which is why they are sometimes used to facilitate anal or vaginal intercourse.

What are the health risks?

Risks include sudden drop in blood pressure (causing fainting or falls), headache, nausea, flushing, and increased heart rate. More serious risks include methemoglobinemia (a blood disorder that reduces oxygen delivery), chemical burns to the skin or nose, and eye damage if the liquid splashes. Deaths have occurred from aspiration (inhaling the liquid), methemoglobinemia, or using poppers with erectile dysfunction drugs like Viagra or Cialis (dangerous blood pressure drop).

Can poppers interact with other drugs?

Yes. Combining poppers with Viagra, Cialis, or similar erectile dysfunction drugs can cause a severe, life-threatening drop in blood pressure. Poppers also interact with blood pressure medications, nitrates (nitroglycerin), and some recreational drugs.

Are poppers legal?

Laws vary by state and country. In the U.S., the FDA banned prescription amyl nitrite except for specific medical use, but industrial alkyl nitrites are often sold under “not for human consumption” labels. Some states have passed laws against selling or possessing poppers. Enforcement is inconsistent, and products remain widely available in gas stations, adult stores, and online.

What does “leather cleaner” or “room odorizer” mean?

Those labels are a legal shield. The products are not actually marketed or sold as effective leather cleaners — they are sold specifically for inhalation as a recreational drug. The warning labels often say “danger if swallowed” but do not warn about the risks of inhaling the vapor.

How can I recognize poppers in a store?

  • Small glass or plastic bottles (usually 10ml–30ml) with names like Jungle Juice, Rush, Super Rush, Hell Fire, Speed, Ecstasy Pop, Amsterdam, Hard, or Iron Horse.
  • Labels that say “leather cleaner,” “room odorizer,” “liquid incense,” “tape head cleaner,” “solvent cleaner,” or “nail polish remover.”
  • Often sold behind the counter, in glass cases, or near the register in adult stores, head shops, and some gas stations or vape shops.
  • Bright colored labels with extreme or party-oriented graphics.

What should I do in an emergency?

If someone swallows poppers, call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222 immediately. If someone collapses, has difficulty breathing, turns blue (methemoglobinemia), or has a seizure, call 911. Do not induce vomiting.

Educational resources for families

How to talk with teens and young adults

  • Explain that poppers are not safe inhalants, even if sold as “cleaners.”
  • Discuss that “not for human consumption” does not mean safe — it is a legal loophole.
  • Explain that mixing poppers with Viagra, Cialis, or blood pressure medicines can cause fainting, stroke, or heart attack.
  • Encourage honest conversations about peer pressure and club drug use.

What to look for in stores

  • Small bottles labeled as leather cleaner, room odorizer, nail polish remover, or solvent cleaner — with brand names like Jungle Juice, Rush, Hell Fire, Speed, or Ecstasy Pop.
  • Products sold in glass display cases near the counter in adult stores, vape shops, or some gas stations.
  • Warning labels that say “fatal if swallowed” or “skin/eye irritant” but are clearly sold for inhalation.
  • Online listings for “poppers” or alkyl nitrites.
